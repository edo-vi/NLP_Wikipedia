{{Music video}}
A '''literal music video''', also called a '''literal video version''', is a satirical remix of an official [[music video]] clip in which the lyrics have been replaced with lyrics that describe the visuals in the video.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://music-mix.ew.com/2009/05/new-literal-vid.html|title=New literal video: "Total Eclipse of the Heart" |last=Greenblatt |first=Leah |date=2009-05-28 |publisher=Entertainment Weekly|access-date=2009-05-31|work=The Music Mix Blog}}</ref>

Literal video versions are usually based on music videos in which the imagery appears illogical, disconnected with the lyrics, and more concerned with impressive visuals than actual meaning.<ref name=literalminded>{{cite web|url=http://www.artsjournal.com/bookdaddy/2008/11/literal-minded.html|title=Literal-minded|last=Weeks|first=Jerome|date=2008-11-03|work=book/daddy|publisher=ArtsJournal|access-date=2009-05-31}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url = http://www.musicradar.com/news/guitars/hot-video-u2s-i-still-havent-found-what-im-looking-for-literal-version-178010 | title = Hot video: U2's I Still Haven't Found What I'm Looking For - literal version | first = Joe | last= Bosso | date = 2008-10-20 | access-date=  2009-08-02 | publisher = [[Music Radar]]}}</ref> Most of the literal videos have been based on music videos from the 1980s and 1990s.<ref name=literalminded/> Literal videos generally have new lyrics dubbed over those of the original video, and often include [[subtitle (captioning)|subtitles]] for better clarity. Lyrics range from references to the [[video editing]] (such as [[Anthony Kiedis]] being dubbed as saying "Now superimpose on me/someone's ugly house" in the [[Red Hot Chili Peppers]]' video for "[[Under the Bridge]]"), to questioning the unusual things depicted in the video which are usually, by convention, ignored by the participants (for example the lyrics "What's happening with that monkey?/What is with this gas mask?/This is a strange library." from the literal version of [[Tears for Fears]]' "[[Head over Heels (Tears for Fears song)|Head Over Heels]]").<ref name=literalminded/><ref>{{cite magazine | url = http://www.time.com/time/arts/article/0,8599,1852733,00.html?iid=fb_share | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20081116121057/http://www.time.com/time/arts/article/0,8599,1852733,00.html?iid=fb_share | url-status = dead | archive-date = November 16, 2008 | title = Tears for Fears: The Literal Remix | magazine = [[Time (magazine)|Time]] | date = 2008-10-28 | access-date=  2009-06-06 | first = Claire | last=  Suddath }}</ref>

The versions of "[[Take On Me]]" and "[[Total Eclipse of the Heart]]" have seen millions of views on [[YouTube]] and have created a brief resurgence of the original song in the popular culture. In the concept's first year, YouTube has hosted 100 different "literal version" videos, from users all over the world, including foreign language entries.<ref>{{cite web | url = http://news.cnet.com/8301-13577_3-10295345-36.html | title = The top 10 songs the Web brought back | date = 2009-07-29 | access-date = 2009-08-02 | publisher = [[CNet]] | first = Caroline | last = Mccarthy }}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.kansascity.com/238/story/1349410.html |title=Literal version videos rack up views on the Web for classic hits |first=James |last=Fussell |date=2009-07-28 |access-date=2009-08-02 |newspaper=[[Kansas City Star]] |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090822185705/http://www.kansascity.com/238/story/1349410.html |archive-date=August 22, 2009 }}</ref>

==Conception==

The first known example of this meme, a redub of [[A-ha]]'s "Take on Me", was posted on YouTube by Dustin McLean in his now-defunct channel Dusto McNeato, in October 2008.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://entertainme.excite.co.uk/news/3685/AHa-get-the-internet-remix-treatment|title=A-Ha get the internet remix treatment|date=2009-10-08|work=Entertainme|publisher=Excite UK|access-date=2009-05-31}}</ref><ref name="rolling stone">{{cite magazine | url = https://www.rollingstone.com/music/news/rocking-literally-the-story-behind-take-on-me-head-over-heels-video-parodies-20081016 | title = Rocking Literally: The Story Behind "Take on Me," "Head Over Heels" Video Parodies | magazine = [[Rolling Stone]] | date = 2008-10-06 | access-date=  2011-03-26 |first =Caryn | last= Ganz }}</ref> McLean, who worked on the animated [[SuperNews!]] show on [[Current TV]], stated that the idea for literal videos came about from an inside joke with his fellow workers,<ref name="rolling stone"/> and that two of his coworkers along with his wife helped to provide the new vocal lyrics.<ref>{{cite web | url = http://www.nbcnews.com/id/27090605 | title = YouTube satire is 'literally' hilarious | publisher = [[NBC News]] | date = 2008-10-06 | access-date = 2009-06-13 | first = Helen A.S. | last = Popkin }}</ref>

In the two months after "Take On Me" was posted, ten other YouTube users began making literal versions of their own. The most popular of these initial non-McLean videos was a redub of [[Rick Astley]]'s "[[Never Gonna Give You Up]]", by copyrighthater, which went on to receive over two million views in its first year online.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Kr2jlCyCJBI |archive-url=https://ghostarchive.org/varchive/youtube/20211220/Kr2jlCyCJBI |archive-date=2021-12-20 |url-status=live|title=Rick Roll: The Literal Version|date=2008-10-17|publisher=YouTube|access-date=2008-10-17}}{{cbignore}}</ref> The new lyrics give a nod to "[[Rickrolling]]", a previous internet meme that involved tricking people into watching the original video of the same song.

==''Total Eclipse of the Heart''==

In the first third of 2009, McLean and seven other people made new literal videos. Five of these were by David A. Scott (dascottjr), a commercial producer in upstate New York. His sixth video, a redub of [[Bonnie Tyler]]'s "[[Total Eclipse of the Heart]]" was posted on May 25, 2009 with the singing done by Scott's friend Felisha Noble.<ref>Laderman, David and Westrup, Laurel.  ''[https://books.google.com/books?id=CkakAgAAQBAJ&pg=PA230 Sampling Media]'', p. 230 (Oxford University Press, 2014).</ref>  Shortly after, links to it appeared on websites for ''[[Entertainment Weekly]]'', the [[Twitter]] page of [[Ashton Kutcher]], and [[Perez Hilton]]'s Twitter page too.  From there, the video received 1,009,331 views in its first ten days, surpassing two million views in three weeks.
<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lj-x9ygQEGA|title=Total Eclipse of the Heart: Literal Video Version|last=Scott|first=David|date=2009-05-25|publisher=YouTube|access-date=2009-05-25}}</ref> After nearly eleven million views, Sony had YouTube block the video worldwide in 2011. (Shortly before his account was terminated in February 2014, the video was unblocked, and surpassed eleven million.) It is still viewable on Funny Or Die's website, where it has received over 100,000 views, qualifying it for "Immortal" status since April 2012.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.funnyordie.com/videos/f03d464867/total-eclipse-of-the-heart-literal-video-version-original |last=Scott |first=David |date=2011-04-16 |title=Total Eclipse of the Heart: Literal Video Version |access-date=2012-07-01}}</ref>

==Copyright issues==

In December 2008, literal versions of "Take On Me" and "Under the Bridge" were removed from Dustin McLean's [[YouTube]] account, due to copyright claims by [[Warner Music Group]]. By that time, "Take On Me" had 2,321,793 views, while "Under the Bridge" had 340,927. Both videos continued to be available on McLean's "[[Funny or Die]]" page, while copied versions were posted by other users on YouTube.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.dustfilms.com|title=Dust Films (official website)|last=McLean|first=Dustin|date=2008-12-12|publisher=Dust Films|access-date=2008-12-12}}</ref>

Two of Scott's literal videos, [[Crowded House]]'s "[[World Where You Live]]" and [[The Killers]]' "[[Read My Mind (The Killers song)|Read My Mind]]" were banned in several non-U.S. countries immediately upon upload, due to copyright restrictions by [[EMI]] and [[Universal Music Group]], respectively. In December 2009, the Killers video was blocked worldwide.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OnPdARocp5Q|title=Total Eclipse Literal Video: Various News Clips|last=Scott|first=David|date=2009-08-24|publisher=YouTube|access-date=2009-08-24}}{{cbignore}}{{Dead Youtube links|date=February 2022}}</ref>

In August 2009, YouTube user Torrey Meeks, a.k.a. levmyshkin (whose May 2008 "Bill O'Reilly Flips Out - DANCE REMIX" got 2.9 million views in its first year) saw his literal version of [[Michael Jackson]]'s "[[Smooth Criminal]]" taken down, after a copyright claim by [[Sony Music]], one month after he had posted it. Meeks contested the claim soon after.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.torreymeeks.com/Site/Home.html|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160303185547/http://www.torreymeeks.com/Site/Home.html|url-status=dead|archive-date=2016-03-03|title=Torrey Meeks (official site)|last=Meeks|first=Torrey|date=2009-08-24|publisher=Torrey Meeks|access-date=2009-08-24}}</ref>

Later that month (less than a week after excerpts were shown on [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]]'s "[[Nightline]]"), the literal videos for "Total Eclipse of the Heart" and [[James Blunt]]'s "[[You're Beautiful]]" (the latter posted by deshem in December 2008) were both removed from YouTube, after a copyright claim by EMI Music. Via YouTube and [[Facebook]], David A. Scott encouraged friends and fans of the videos to complain about the ban to YouTube and EMI.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.weny.com/News-Local.asp?ARCH3864=1&ARTICLE3864=9147225|title=Literal Video Unbanned|last=Fioraliso|first=Ted|date=2009-08-26|publisher=Facebook|access-date=2009-08-26|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110717135327/http://www.weny.com/News-Local.asp?ARCH3864=1&ARTICLE3864=9147225|archive-date=2011-07-17}}</ref>

Before Scott could contact YouTube or EMI himself, both videos were restored to their original accounts on August 26, only two days after they were banned. YouTube and EMI gave no reason for the reversal.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rDTNEZMjz0E|title=Total Eclipse Literal Video: UNBANNED|last=Scott|first=David|date=2009-08-29|publisher=YouTube|access-date=2009-08-29}}{{cbignore}}{{Dead Youtube links|date=February 2022}}</ref> That same day, YouTube also unbanned McLean's videos of "Take On Me" and "Under the Bridge", without saying why. Also that day, YouTube accepted Meeks' counter-notification, restoring his video of "Smooth Criminal". However, Sony had Meeks' video removed again by September 1. By the end of December, all but one of Meeks' six literal videos were removed, due to copyright claims by UMG. McLean's "Under the Bridge" was blocked again in 2010.

In November 2009, CeilingofStars posted a literal version of [[Lady Gaga]]'s "[[Bad Romance]]". The video was automatically blocked by YouTube for containing material owned by UMG. The copyright claim was contested, and the video reappeared online, shortly before being removed by Universal. Around the same time, Warner Music Group had cushlinkes' literal version of "Whip It" removed as well. By the end of December 2009, over twenty of the 100 English language literal videos had been removed from YouTube, mostly due to copyright claims from Universal Music Group and Sony Music. Artists parodied by the removed videos included [[Billy Joel]], [[Boyz II Men]], REM, Lady Gaga, [[Britney Spears]], [[Hanson (band)|Hanson]], [[Cyndi Lauper]], and Rick Astley. Some of the removed videos have been re-uploaded on other video websites. Record companies, claiming copyright infringement, would continue to block and remove literal videos from YouTube.

According to McLean, various networks ([[MTV]], [[VH1]], and [[CMT (U.S. TV channel)|CMT]]) have contacted him about turning the concept into a TV program. However, the difficulty in getting the rights to original music videos has scared off most developers.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://blogs.wsj.com/speakeasy/2009/07/28/dustin-mclean-literal-video-mastermind-on-writing-legal-woes-and-his-go-to-karoke-song/tab/article/|title=Dustin McLean, Literal Video Mastermind|last=Kennison|first=James|date=2009-07-28|newspaper=Wall Street Journal|access-date=2009-10-19}}</ref>

In February 2014, YouTube terminated David A. Scott's entire YouTube account, citing "multiple third-party notifications of copyright infringement".<ref>{{Cite web | url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=lj-x9ygQEGA | title=- YouTube | website=[[YouTube]] }}</ref> He had received two previous, expired copyright strikes in April 2012, for his Killers and [[Mika (singer)|Mika]] videos (both by UMG). The three strikes he received in 2014 were for videos by [[The Beatles]] (Apple Corps), [[Crowded House]] (UMG), and [[Nirvana (band)|Nirvana]] (UMG).

In 2014, Scott uploaded all of his literal videos to his [[Funny or Die]] channel.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.funnyordie.com/dascottjr|title = Unauthorized Access}}</ref> In 2018, Funny or Die moved all of its site's uploaded videos onto a YouTube server, causing four of Scott's videos to be removed (and redirected to different videos) by YouTube's copyright detection. At the same time, Funny or Die announced they would "no longer support open [[user-generated content]]", meaning that Scott would no longer be able to edit his Funny or Die page or upload new material.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.funnyordie.com/2018/8/20/17784670/an-important-note-about-funny-or-die-from-the-folks-at-funny-or-die|title = Unauthorized Access| date=20 August 2018 }}</ref>

* Less than two weeks after Dustin McLean's literal version of Beck's "Loser" video was posted on [[Funny or Die]] (and later on YouTube), Beck himself featured it on his official website.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://beck.com/|title=Beck (Official Website)|last=Hansen|first=Beck|date=2009-08-09|publisher=XL Recordings|access-date=2009-08-09|archive-date=2009-08-06|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090806235143/http://beck.com/|url-status=dead}}</ref> This did not keep his record company (UMG) from blocking the video on YouTube the following year.
