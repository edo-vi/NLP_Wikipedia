The '''Page 99 test''' is a method of evaluating a work of [[fiction]] suggested by literary critic [[Ford Madox Ford]].<ref>{{cite web|last1=Mangan|first1=Lucy|title=Putting the Page 99 test to the, er, test|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/2010/sep/27/page-99-test-book|website=The Guardian|accessdate=11 May 2015|date=September 27, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last1=McCrum|first1=Robert|title=What's the best way to test a novel before you read it?|url=https://www.theguardian.com/books/booksblog/2010/sep/28/test-novel-before-you-read|website=The Guardian|accessdate=11 May 2015|date=September 28, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last1=East|first1=Ben|title=Putting books to the page 99 test|url=http://www.thenational.ae/arts-culture/books/putting-books-to-the-page-99-test|website=The National|accessdate=11 May 2015|date=October 19, 2010}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|last1=Henig|first1=Robin Marantz|title=The Page 99 Test|url=https://www.psychologytoday.com/blog/cusp/201211/the-page-99-test|website=Psychology Today|accessdate=11 May 2015|date=November 14, 2012}}</ref>  
Ford suggested that prospective readers open a book and read page 99 to gain a sense of how well written the work is while avoiding any back-cover synopsis or the first few pages, as these are typically given extra attention during editing and may not reflect the quality of the book as a whole.
