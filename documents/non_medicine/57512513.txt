'''''Crying for the Carolines''''' is a 1930 short film made by [[Leon Schlesinger]].<ref name="silverhalfcrown 2009">{{Citation|last=silverhalfcrown|title=SM001 Crying for the Carolines (Dec., 1930)|date=August 22, 2009|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SBo98gjikxQ|access-date=May 25, 2018}}</ref> The theme for this short film is played on the [[Wurlitzer]] organ formerly installed at [[Warner Brothers]] studios by Milton Charles,<ref name="silverhalfcrown 2009" /><ref name="Bradley 2009">{{Cite book|url=https://play.google.com/books/reader?id=M7vwCQAAQBAJ&printsec=frontcover&output=reader&hl=en_GB&pg=GBS.PA444|title=The First Hollywood Sound Shorts, 1926–1931|last=Bradley|first=Edwin M.|date=April 27, 2009|publisher=McFarland|isbn=9781476606842|language=en}}</ref> a theatre organist during the silent era of film.<ref>{{Cite news|url=http://articles.latimes.com/1991-11-07/news/mn-1421_1_milton-charles|title=Milton Charles; Organist for Silent Movies, Radio Shows|last=FOLKART|first=BURT A.|date=November 7, 1991|work=Los Angeles Times|access-date=May 25, 2018|issn=0458-3035}}</ref> He is dubbed the 'Singing Organist' in the film.<ref name="Bradley 2009" />

The film, which was recorded on [[Western Electric]] apparatus,<ref name="silverhalfcrown 2009" /> was the only one made in a planned series called ''Spooney Melodies''. The film is a music video to advertise the song sung by Charles. The organ music was written by [[Frank Marsales]], who would go on to write the musical arrangements for the [[Merrie Melodies]] series, including the cartoon ''[[Smile, Darn Ya, Smile!]]''

==Plot==
The films open, with art deco style animation, set to organ music. After, some animation. It cuts to Charles playing the organ and singing ''Cryin' for the Carolines'', a song written by [[Harry Warren]], [[Sam M. Lewis|Sam Lewis]], and [[Joe Young (lyricist)|Joe Young]] the same year. The film's animation which is shown throughout the film includes, a forest, a city and a sun shining over a country plain. Milton Charles is portrayed in voice and live action footage throughout the short, as he sings the song.<ref name="silverhalfcrown 2009" />

== Background ==
The song, ''Cryin' for the Carolines'', is originally featured in the 1930 [[Warner Bros.]] film ''[[Spring is Here (film)|Spring is Here]]'', which was issued on the sound system [[Vitaphone]],<ref name="silverhalfcrown 2009" /> which was accomplished with a [[record player]] which played a disk in time with the projector,<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://silentfilm.org/archive/vitaphone-vaudeville-1926-1930|title=Vitaphone Vaudeville, 1926–1930 |website=Silent Film |access-date=May 25, 2018}}</ref> which would be higher quality than [[sound-on-film]]. In the film, the song was sung by [[The Brox Sisters]].<ref>{{Citation|last=rrgomes|title=The Brox Sisters: Crying for the Carolines (1930)|date=May 4, 2010|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=seQRlnczr3E|access-date=May 25, 2018}}</ref>

== Availability ==
The planned series was short-lived, with [[Warner Bros.|Warner Brothers]] instead going for the [[Merrie Melodies]] series, beginning with 1931's ''[[Lady, Play Your Mandolin!]]''. This short is available on the Looney Tunes Golden Collection: Volume 6's 3rd disc.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.dvdtalk.com/reviews/35354/looney-tunes-golden-collection-vol-6/|title=Looney Tunes: Golden Collection, Vol. 6|website=DVD Talk|access-date=May 25, 2018}}</ref>

== Reception ==
''Crying for the Carolines'' was reviewed by the magazine ''Photoplay'' in its 1930 December issue.<ref name="Bradley 2009" /><ref name="Photoplay Magazine Publishing Company 1930">{{Cite book|url=https://archive.org/details/photoplay3839movi|title=Photoplay (Jul–Dec 1930)|last=Photoplay Magazine Publishing Company|date=1930|publisher=Chicago, Photoplay Magazine Publishing Company|others=PFA Library and Film Study Center UC Berkeley Art Museum and Pacific Film Archive}}</ref> The magazine spoke positively about the film, citing that the film is a "distinct relief from the monotony of many sound shorts".<ref name="Photoplay Magazine Publishing Company 1930" /> The magazine also said that the short is notable for the "beauty of the results obtained", from the painting and the drawings featured in the film, "as well as the novelty of the film."<ref name="Photoplay Magazine Publishing Company 1930" />
