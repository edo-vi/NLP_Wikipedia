{{short description|Bacterial disease of sheep}}
'''Braxy''' is an infectious disease which causes sudden death in [[sheep]]. It is caused by the [[bacterium]] ''[[Clostridium septicum]]''.

Braxy generally occurs in winter, when sheep eat frosted root crops,<ref name="Scott 2015"/> or frosted grass.<ref name="Winter 2012">{{cite book|last1=Winter|first1=AC|title=A handbook for the sheep clinician|date=2012|publisher=CABI|location=Wallingford|isbn=9781845939939|pages=92–93|edition=7th|chapter=Chapter 10: Sudden death: Main clostridial diseases}}</ref> The frozen feed damages the mucosa (lining) of the [[abomasum]], allowing ''C.&nbsp;septicum'' to enter, causing abomasitis and a fatal [[bacteremia]].<ref>{{cite book|last1=Songer|first1=JG|editor1-last=Gyles|editor1-first=CL|editor2-last=Prescott|editor2-first=JF|editor3-last=Songer|editor3-first=JG|editor4-last=Thoen|editor4-first=CO|title=Pathogenesis of bacterial infections in animals|date=2010|publisher=Wiley-Blackwell|location=Ames, Iowa|isbn=978-0-8138-1237-3|pages=203–209|edition=4th|chapter=Chapter 12: Histotoxic Clostridia}}</ref>

Young sheep not protected with a [[vaccine]] are most commonly affected. If sheep are not found dead, signs include abdominal pain and recumbency.<ref name="Winter 2012"/> There is no treatment,<ref name="Scott 2015"/> and sheep usually die within 36 hours of the onset of signs.<ref name="Prescott 2016">{{cite book|last1=Prescott|first1=JF|last2=Menzies|first2=PI|last3=Fraser|first3=RS|editor1-last=Uzal|editor1-first=FA|editor2-last=Songer|editor2-first=JG|editor3-last=Prescott|editor3-first=JF|editor4-last=Popoff|editor4-first=MR|title=Clostridial diseases of animals|date=2016|publisher=John Wiley & Sons|isbn=9781118728307|pages=205–220|chapter=Chapter 17: Clostridial abomasitis}}</ref> The carcass of sheep which died of braxy will often decompose more rapidly than expected.<ref name="Scott 2015">{{cite book|last1=Scott|first1=PR|title=Sheep medicine|date=2015|publisher=CRC Press|isbn=9781498700153|page=137|edition=2nd|chapter=Digestive system: Braxy}}</ref>

Historically, the [[mutton]] of affected sheep was also referred to as braxy.<ref>{{Cite Nuttall|title=Braxy}}</ref>

A vaccine against braxy was developed at the [[Moredun Research Institute]] in Scotland.<ref>{{cite journal|title=Phone call reveals link to Moredun's past|journal=Moredun Magazine|date=2013|issue=6|pages=1|url=http://www.moredun.org.uk/webfm_send/579|access-date=2016-08-04|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160821154717/http://www.moredun.org.uk/webfm_send/579#|archive-date=2016-08-21|url-status=dead}}</ref>

Braxy has been reported in Europe (particularly in Iceland, Norway and the UK), Australia and the United States.<ref>{{cite journal|last1=Songer|first1=JG|title=Clostridial diseases of small ruminants.|journal=Veterinary Research|date=1998|volume=29|issue=3–4|pages=219–32|pmid=9689739}}</ref>
