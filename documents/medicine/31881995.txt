'''Sir William MacGregor Henderson''' [[Fellow of the Royal Society|FRS]]<ref name="Boden">{{Cite journal | last1 = Boden | first1 = Edward | doi = 10.1098/rsbm.2004.0010 | title = Sir William Macgregor Henderson. 17 July 1913 – 29 November 2000: Elected F.R.S. 1976 | journal = [[Biographical Memoirs of Fellows of the Royal Society]] | volume = 50 | pages = 133–146 | year = 2004 | pmid =  15754472| doi-access = free }}</ref> [[FRSE]] MRCVS (17 July 1913 – 29 November 2000) was a Scottish [[Veterinarian|veterinary]] expert on [[Foot-and-mouth disease|foot and mouth disease]].  He was also President of the [[Zoological Society of London]], 1984–1989.<ref name="guardian">{{Cite web|url=https://www.theguardian.com/news/2000/dec/19/guardianobituaries2|title=Obituary - Sir William Henderson - His researches checked the scourge of foot and mouth disease|date=19 December 2000|access-date=25 May 2011|work=[[The Guardian]]|first=Walter |last=Plowright}}</ref> He was in charge of controlling foot and mouth disease in South America from 1957 to 1966. In  his life he was generally called '''Gregor Henderson'''.

==Life==
Henderson was born in Edinburgh, the son of William Simpson Henderson and his wife, Catherine Alice Marcus Berry.<ref name="BioIndex">{{cite book|title=Biographical Index of Former Fellows of the Royal Society of Edinburgh 1783–2002|date=July 2006|publisher=The Royal Society of Edinburgh|isbn=0-902-198-84-X|url=https://www.royalsoced.org.uk/cms/files/fellows/biographical_index/fells_indexp1.pdf}}</ref> His father was managing director of the company which printed bank-notes for the [[Royal Bank of Scotland]].<ref name="Telegraph" /> They lived at 123 Dalkeith Road in the south of the city.<ref>Edinburgh and Leith Directory 1911-12</ref> He was educated at [[George Watson's College]] in [[Edinburgh]].

He studied at the [[Royal (Dick) School of Veterinary Studies|Royal (Dick) Veterinary College]] in [[Edinburgh]].  During his  career at the [[Institute for Animal Health|Animal Virus Research Institute]] at [[Pirbright]], which he joined in 1939, Henderson developed an improved foot and mouth vaccine and developed a method of determining the virus content in a sample of infected material, which became known as the '''Henderson Method'''. He obtained his DSc from the University of Edinburgh in 1945 with a thesis entitled “The quantitative study of foot-and-mouth disease virus”.<ref>{{Cite journal |last=Henderson |first=William MacGregor |date=1945 |title=The quantitative study of foot-and-mouth disease virus |url=https://era.ed.ac.uk/handle/1842/34653 |language=en}}</ref> Henderson remained at Pirbright until 1956.<ref name="Telegraph">{{cite web|url=https://www.telegraph.co.uk/news/obituaries/1377574/Sir-William-Henderson.html|title=Obituary Sir William Henderson|work=[[The Daily Telegraph]]|date=9 December 2000|access-date=25 May 2011}}</ref>

From 1972 to 1978, he was Secretary of the Agricultural Research Council. He was knighted in 1976.
Henderson was elected as a Fellow of the [[Royal Society]] in 1976<ref name="guardian" /> and a Fellow of the [[Royal Society of Edinburgh]] in 1977. His proposers for the latter were Sir [[William Weipers]], Sir John N Ritchie, Sir Alexander Robertson, and Robert Comline.<ref name="BioIndex" />

Following retirement he became the Director of [[London Zoo]].<ref name="guardian" />

==Family==

In 1941 he married Alys Beryl Goodridge; they had four sons.<ref name="Boden" />
